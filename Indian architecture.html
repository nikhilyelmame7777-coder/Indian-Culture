Ancient Architecture

Ancient architecture, spanning from early civilizations to roughly 550 CE, is characterized by monumental stone structures, rock-cut caves, and early temples designed around religious and cosmic principles. Key features include advanced urban planning (Indus Valley), monumental columns, and meticulous craftsmanship, with structures often built to showcase royal power or spiritual devotion.

Ancient Indian Architecture:

  • Mauryan & Gupta Periods: Defined by monumental stone structures, rock-cut caves (Ajanta and Ellora), and refined temples (5th-century Gupta temples).
  • Temple Design: Cities were often designed around a central temple, acting as a spiritual and social hub.
  • Key Features: Stupas (e.g., Sanchi), free-standing circular, apsidal, and shikhara-style temples, and rock-cut architecture.
  • Other Structures: Stepwells, such as the 11th-century Rani ki Vav in Gujarat, highlight incredible subterranean engineering.

Bronze Age Period

Period (5th–2nd Millennium BCE) Early Harappan phase Although the urban phase of Harappa has been dated back to 2600 BCE, excavation at Kalibangan from the early or proto-Harappan period already shows an urban development with fortification, grid layout of the city and drain system. The settlement consisted of a fortified city mostly made of mud-brick architecture but characterized by an appearance of fired bricks around 3000 BCE which was used to line the drains of the city. Planned settlements from an early Harappan era with structures parallel to the streets which run perpendicular to each other with public drainage system has been uncovered at the site of Rakhigarhi, one of the biggest urbanized areas of the Indus Valley Civilization dating back to 4000–3200 BCE. Even earlier phase dated 4400–4200 BCE has marked the appearance of wedge-shaped mud bricks with rectangular houses.[6]

Classical period (320 BCE - 550 CE)

Monumental stone architecture The Indian emperor Ashoka (rule: 273-232 BCE) established the Pillars of Ashoka throughout his realm, generally next to Buddhist stupas. According to Buddhist tradition, Ashoka recovered the relics of the Buddha from the earlier stupas (except from the Ramagrama stupa), and erected 84.000 stupas to distribute the relics across India. In effect, many stupas are thought to date originally from the time of Ashoka, such as Sanchi or Kesariya, where he also erected pillars with his inscriptions, and possibly Bharhut, Amaravati or Dharmarajika in Gandhara.

Evidence indicates that the construction of fortification walls at Delhi applied nearly the same principle at Red Fort and Agra Fort as was the tradition during pre-Islamic Rajput periods. Temple Architecture

Introduction

Most of the architectural remains that survive from Ancient and Medieval India are religious in nature. In different parts of the country, distinct architectural style of temples was result of geographical, ethnic and historical diversities.

Two broad orders of temples in the country are known as Nagara in the north and Dravida in the south. At times, the Vesara style of temples is also found as an independent style, created through the selective mixing of the Nagara and Dravida orders.

As temples grew more complex, more surfaces were created for sculpture by adding more and more rhythmically projecting, symmetrical walls and niches, without breaking away from the fundamental plan of the shrine.

Basic Features of the Hindu Temples:

  • Sanctum (garbhagriha literally ‘womb-house’): It was a small cubicle with a single entrance which grew into a larger chamber in time. The garbhagriha is made to house the main icon.
  • Entrance to the temple: It may be a portico or colonnaded hall that incorporates space for a large number of worshippers and is known as a mandapa.
  • Freestanding temples tend to have a mountain-like spire: It can take the shape of a curving shikhar in North India and a pyramidal tower, called a vimana, in South India.
  • The vahan: It was mount or vehicle of the temple’s main deity along with a standard pillar or dhvaj is placed axially before the sanctum.
  • Mithun sculptures: Many Hindu temples feature mithun (embracing couple) sculptures, considered auspicious. Usually, they are placed at the entrance of the temple or on an exterior wall or they may also be placed on the walls between the mandapa and the main shrine.

Nagara or North Indian Temple Style

In North India, temples are usually built on a raised stone platform with steps. Unlike South India, they generally lack large boundary walls or gateways. The main shrine (garbhagriha) lies directly under the tallest tower called shikhara.

  • Latina: Square base with inward curving tower.
  • Phamsana: Broader and shorter with sloping roof.
  • Valabhi: Rectangular with vaulted roof.

Central Indian Temples

Temples in Uttar Pradesh, Madhya Pradesh, and Rajasthan are mostly made of sandstone. Gupta period temples are simple with pillars and small mandapas. Deogarh temple is a classic example of the panchayatana style.

  • Khajuraho temples are famous for sculptures.
  • Kandariya Mahadeo temple is a major example.

Western Indian Temples

Temples in Gujarat and Rajasthan use sandstone, basalt, and marble. The Sun Temple at Modhera is famous for its large stepped tank and alignment with sunlight.

Eastern Indian Temples

Eastern temples are found in Bengal, Odisha, and Assam. Terracotta was widely used in Bengal, while Odisha temples are known for detailed carvings.

  • Konark Sun Temple: Designed like a chariot with stone wheels.
  • Kamakhya Temple: Important Shakti Peeth in Assam.

Hill Temples

Temples in Kashmir and Himalayas show a mix of wooden architecture and stone styles. Many temples combine Nagara features with local traditions.

Dravida or South Indian Temple Style

Dravida temples are enclosed by walls with large gateways called gopurams. The main tower (vimana) is pyramid-shaped, and temple complexes often include water tanks.

  • Famous temple cities: Kanchipuram, Madurai, Thanjavur.
  • Brihadeeswara temple is one of the largest temples in India.

Deccan Architecture

The Deccan region shows a mix of North and South styles called Vesara. The Kailashnath temple at Ellora is a masterpiece carved from a single rock.

Buddhist Architecture

Bodhgaya and Nalanda were major Buddhist centres. The Mahabodhi Temple reflects a mix of Nagara and Dravida styles.

Jain Architecture

Jain temples are found across India. The Dilwara temples at Mount Abu and the statue of Gomateshwara at Shravanabelagola are famous examples.

1.Lotus Temple

The Lotus Temple is a Baháʼí House of Worship in New Delhi, India. It was completed in December 1986 and is notable for its lotus-like shape, making it a prominent attraction in the city.

Like all Bahá’í Houses of Worship, the Lotus Temple is open to all people, regardless of religion or any other qualification.

  • 👨‍🎨 Architect: Fariborz Sahba
  • 🏗️ Architectural Style: Expressionist architecture
  • 📅 Opened: 24 December 1986

2. Chandigarh Capitol Complex

The Chandigarh Capitol Complex is located in Sector 1, Chandigarh, India. It was designed in 1952 by the famous Swiss-French architect Le Corbusier. In 2016, it was recognized as a UNESCO World Heritage Site as part of “The Architectural Work of Le Corbusier”.

  • 📍 Location: Sector 1, Chandigarh, India
  • 👨‍🎨 Architect: Le Corbusier
  • 📅 Year of Design: 1952
  • 🌍 UNESCO Status: World Heritage Site (2016)

✨ Key Components:

  • 🏛️ Palace of Assembly: Monumental structure for legislative functions.
  • 🏢 Secretariat Building: Large administrative block with modular design.
  • ⚖️ High Court: Known for its dramatic roof structure.
  • 👐 Open Hand Monument: Symbol of peace — “open to give, open to receive”.

The complex reflects modernist principles such as exposed concrete, bold geometry, and planned urban design. It represents the three pillars of democracy—executive, legislative, and judicial.

It is an important symbol of India’s post-independence development and remains a major tourist attraction in Chandigarh.

3. Mumbai Skyline

Mumbai’s skyline is a striking symbol of India’s rapid urban growth and modern architectural ambition. Once known for colonial and Art Deco buildings, the city now features glass-and-steel skyscrapers, luxury towers, and advanced commercial hubs.

🌆 Key Features:

  • 🏙️ Mixed-use complexes (offices + retail + residences)
  • 🌊 Iconic skyline along the Arabian Sea
  • 🌍 Global architectural influence
  • ✨ Luxury living with modern amenities

🏙 Notable Skyscrapers:
